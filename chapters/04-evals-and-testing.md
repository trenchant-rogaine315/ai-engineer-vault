# Chapter 04: Evals & Testing

> [!TIP]
> **TL;DR**
> - Traditional NLP metrics (BLEU, ROUGE) are useless for complex reasoning tasks.
> - "LLM-as-a-Judge" is the industry standard but requires strict bias mitigation (calibration).
> - Golden Datasets (ground truth) are the most valuable asset in your evaluation pipeline.
> - Evals must be integrated into CI/CD as automated regression tests.

## What this chapter covers
In AI Engineering, "it looks right" is not a valid test result. Because LLMs are non-deterministic, you must implement a rigorous, mathematical framework for evaluating system performance. 

This chapter formalizes the transition from manual "vibes-based" testing to automated, metric-driven evaluation. You will learn how to build domain-specific evaluation datasets, calibrate automated judges to eliminate verbosity and position bias, and integrate continuous quality measurement directly into your deployment cycle. We focus on techniques that allow you to ship with confidence, knowing exactly how a model update or prompt change impacts your system's accuracy.

## When you need this
- **Use this when**: You are preparing to move an AI system from prototype to production and need to guarantee reliability.
- **Do NOT use this when**: You are in the early experimental phase and the cost of building an evaluation suite outweighs the speed of iteration.

## Core Concepts

### The Death of Deterministic Unit Tests
Traditional unit tests (expect A to equal B) fail in the domain of generative AI because there are infinite ways to phrase a correct answer. Instead, we move toward "Linguistic Unit Testing" and "Semantic Validation." This involves testing for specific properties: Does the answer contain the required IDs? Is the JSON schema valid? Is the medical or legal advice factually grounded? You are testing for "Behavioral Bounds" rather than exact string parity.

### Building the "Golden Dataset"
The foundation of any evaluation pipeline is the "Golden Dataset"—a curated collection of high-quality input-output pairs that represent the ground truth for your specific domain. This dataset should not be generated by an AI alone; it requires human expert verification. As you encounter production failures, you should harvest those cases and add them to your Golden Dataset to prevent regressions. This dataset becomes your most valuable intellectual property, as it is the only way to prove your system is better than a generic baseline.

### "LLM-as-a-Judge" and Calibration
The most effective way to scale evaluation is to use a high-powered model (e.g., GPT-4o or Claude 3.5 Sonnet) to grade the outputs of your production model. However, automated judges are prone to systematic biases: they prefer longer answers (verbosity bias), they are influenced by which answer comes first (position bias), and they often favor their own writing style (self-preference bias). Calibration involves running your judge against a set of human-graded examples to calculate its "Bias Offset" and applying mathematical corrections to its scores.

### Integration into the Development Lifecycle (CI/Eval)
Evaluation should not be a one-off event. It must be integrated into your CI/CD pipeline. Every time a prompt is edited or a model version is changed, the evaluation suite should trigger automatically. This "CI/Eval" loop provides instant feedback to developers, demonstrating whether a change improved context recall or inadvertently increased hallucination rates.

## Common Failure Modes

### 1. The Verbosity Bias Trap
- **Symptom**: Your evaluation metrics show a massive improvement, but human users complain that the answers have become bloated and less helpful.
- **Root Cause**: Your automated judge is rewarding the model for "looking authoritative" through length rather than for actual factual accuracy.
- **Fix**: Update your evaluation rubric to explicitly penalize verbosity and use "Pairwise Comparison" where the judge must choose between a long and a short answer with identical facts.

### 2. High Variance in Automated Scores
- **Symptom**: Running the same evaluation suite twice yields significantly different results.
- **Root Cause**: The evaluation model itself is behaving non-deterministically or the rubric is too vague.
- **Fix**: Set the evaluation model's `temperature` to 0 and refine the rubric into granular, binary questions (e.g., "Does the response cite a specific page number? Yes/No") rather than 1-10 scales.

### 3. Over-fitting to the Eval Set
- **Symptom**: System scores 99% on the evaluation set but fails on real-world queries from new users.
- **Root Cause**: The evaluation dataset is too narrow and doesn't represent the diversity of actual user behavior.
- **Fix**: Implement "Production Shadowing," where a small percentage of real user queries are sampled and added to a "Dynamic Eval Set" for continuous pipeline testing.
